National Outdoor Leadership School

At the age of 10, I first walked into my local climbing gym for my birthday party, though if you asked me at the time I would have never gone back. However, at the suggestion of my father I eventually returned, and slowly he helped me foster a love for rock climbing that persists to this day. When I was 16, after 5 years of absorbing all the technical knowledge I could from the outdoor guides who ran the climbing gym, I embarked on a 30 day rock climbing and backpacking trip with the National Outdoor Leadership School (NOLS) in the backcountry of the Wind River Range in WY. While 5 years of outdoor climbing had prepared me well for the technical goals of the course, the trip was incredibly influential in my development as a leader, team member, problem solver and critical thinker, and provided me with the confidence, personal and team skills, and drive to excel not only in my climbing, but in my professional endeavors as well.

Clarkson University Outing Club

When I enrolled at Clarkson, it was not the engineering program that truly drew me to the small university in Upstate New York. Instead, I was enthralled when I met the university's Outing Club, and the seemingless endless number of likeminded outdoor enthusiasts. Coupled with the location, which was only a few hours north of my local Adirondack Mountains, the Outing Club provided the perfect respite from the stresses of college. In addition, it allowed me an opportunity to continue my favorite aspect(?) of climbing, which was teaching others about safely and efficiently climbing in the outdoors. I immediately began teaching fellow club members the guiding, climbing, and rescue skills that I had learned, before being elected as one of the officers for the climbing cage of our club. In conjunction with that, I worked as a route setter at our university's new climbing wall, continuing my love for sharing climbing with every new climber who walked through the door.

Robotic Handover Project

When I joined the TARS Lab at Clarkson, I was first assigned to help the graduate students who were working on the Robotic Handover Project, which was a computer vision project designed to better understand human to human handover. The goal of this was to help produce seamless robot to human handover, which was intuitive and natural for the human. During my time on the project, I assisted with the assembly of one of the many iterations of a cubic capture rig which gathered data from over 10 high speed cameras across 3 modalities. In addition, I helped run experiments in which subjects would pass common household objects between each other. Following that, I assisted in the annotation of the capture data, completing various tasks such as key frame annotation. Although I did less data intensive tasks on the robotic handover project, my time with the project was monumental in building my appreciation for working with large data, and the intricacies of maintaining control and accuracy in running the experiment, as well as the massive computational load that working with computer vision data demanded.

Broken Object Repair

After my work on the Robotic Handover Project, I was offered the opportunity to work full time for a summer at the lab, assisting with a project which was developing algorithms aimed at taking dense 3d scans of broken, everyday objects, and producing 3d printed repair parts for the object. My work here was more personalized, and I was set in charge of growing the dataset of both complete object 3d scans, as well as fractured and broken 3d scans of objects set to be repaired. Furthermore, I was in charge of the organization and maintaining of the dataset of hundreds of 3d models, and much of my work involved pre and post processing of the objects and models. My efforts there culminated in writing and being named an author on the project's dataset paper which earned acceptance at the IEEE/CVF Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition, the highest ranking computer science venue in the world, and the 4th most cited research publication. This project was incredibly influential in my development as a computer science student, and only grew my appreciation for the development of and effort working with large datasets, as well as the beauty in drawing meaningful insights from incredibly dense and intensive forms of data.
